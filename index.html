Key Cross-Study Findings

  • Three universal levers reduce calorie intake across every dietary pattern: lower energy density, slower eating, fewer hyper-palatable foods. Hall et al., 2019; Rolls et al., 1999; Fazzino et al., 2023
  • People eat ~30% more when given larger portions — without feeling more full or even noticing. Rolls et al., 2002 — controlled experiment, n=51
  • Each daily SSB increases child obesity risk by 60% and doubles adult diabetes risk at 1+ servings/day. Brownell et al., 2009; Vartanian et al., 2007 — 88-study meta-analysis
  • Fast food ≥2x/week over 15 years: +4.5 kg weight gain and 2x insulin resistance vs. <1x/week. Pereira et al., 2005 — 15-year cohort, n=3,031
  • No country has successfully reversed its obesity epidemic through public health measures alone. Swinburn et al., 2011 — The Lancet

* Wansink data caveat: Brian Wansink’s lab was found guilty of research misconduct by Cornell University in 2018, resulting in 18 retractions across his body of work. These two specific papers have not been retracted, but both have documented issues:

  • “Bottomless Bowls” (Study 7): Forensic analysis by James Heathers (SPRITE method) identified statistically impossible values — including cell sizes incompatible with the described experimental design. However, a large preregistered replication (Lopez et al., 2024; Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, n=464) confirmed the core effect: people with self-refilling bowls did eat significantly more without noticing. The effect size was roughly half of Wansink’s original claim (d=0.45 vs. d=0.84).
  • “200 Daily Food Decisions” (Study 8): Researchers at the Max Planck Institute (Claassen, Hertwig, & Mata, 2025; Appetite) demonstrated that the “200+” figure is a measurement artifact caused by the subadditivity effect — a known cognitive bias where estimating subcategories separately and summing them yields inflated totals. The specific number should not be cited as an empirical finding.

We retain both studies because the underlying concepts — visual cues influence how much we eat, and people are poor judges of their own consumption — are supported by independent evidence. But the specific numbers from these papers should be treated with caution.

Evidence Strength

We distinguish between types of evidence and always tell you which kind backs each claim:

  • Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) — Gold standard. Participants randomly assigned to conditions. Can establish causation. Example: Hall et al. (2019) NIH inpatient trial.
  • Meta-Analysis — Statistical synthesis of multiple studies. Strongest when including RCTs. Example: Vartanian et al. (2007), 88 studies on soft drinks.
  • Prospective Cohort — Large groups followed over time. Strong associational evidence. Example: Pereira et al. (2005), 3,031 adults over 15 years.
  • Cross-Sectional / Observational — Snapshot of populations. Can reveal patterns but not causation. Example: Nielsen & Popkin (2003), national portion size surveys.
  • Modeled / Policy Analysis — Projections based on data. Useful for policy design. Example: Brownell et al. (2009), SSB tax revenue estimates.

What We Don’t Have

Transparency about limitations is as important as the evidence itself:

  • The landmark UPF trial (Hall 2019) was only 2 weeks per arm — long-term RCT data on ultra-processed food doesn’t yet exist.
  • Fast food evidence comes primarily from one (excellent) 15-year study.
  • Two Wansink studies are included with data integrity caveats throughout.
  • One study (Brownell & Horgen, 2003) is a book — no digital full text is available.
  • One study (Fazzino, 2023) was too recent for full text access — we rely on the abstract and press releases.
  • These studies do not cover microbiome effects, mental health connections, specific food additives, or cultural variation beyond the US, Brazil, UK, and Australia.

How the Quiz Scoring Works

The Food Score is calculated from 12 questions across 5 evidence-based categories: Ultra-Processed Food Exposure, Liquid Calories, Portion Habits, Food Environment, and Protective Habits. Each question maps to specific study findings. Your answers are scored 0–10, totaled, and normalized to a 0–100 scale. Category breakdowns show which areas of your diet carry the most risk — and the most opportunity for improvement — according to the research.

This is an educational tool, not medical advice. Consult a healthcare professional for personalized dietary guidance.
